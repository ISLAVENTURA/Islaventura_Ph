Walking Through Time: A Weekend in Vigan, Ilocos Sur


One quiet weekend in May, we decided to trade city noise for cobblestones and ancestral homes. Vigan was calling, and we were ready to be transported. The drive up North was long but never dull—fields stretching endlessly, roadside empanada stalls popping up in sleepy towns, and the occasional carabao crossing to keep you alert. By the time we reached Vigan, the light was soft and golden, casting a glow on streets that seemed untouched by time. The first thing that hit us? The sound of hooves. Not traffic, not engines—hooves. We dropped our bags and immediately walked to Calle Crisologo, where every step echoed like a footnote from the past. Spanish-era houses stood proud on either side, their capiz windows catching the light, their wooden balconies draped in bougainvillea. It felt like stepping into a sepia photograph, only warmer.

We spent the afternoon exploring on foot—ducking into antique shops, trying Vigan longganisa straight from the grill, and sipping cold sugarcane juice to beat the heat. At Syquia Mansion, we walked through rooms where silence spoke louder than tour guides. Over at Plaza Salcedo, we sat by the fountain and watched the sun dip behind the cathedral, casting long shadows that stretched like stories across the square.

Dinner was at a carinderia tucked into a side street, run by a Lola who insisted we try her pinakbet with bagnet—crispy, rich, and unforgettable. Later that night, the dancing fountain show lit up the plaza with music and color, locals and travelers clapping in rhythm. We just stood there, letting it all soak in—the sound of Ilocano songs, the breeze, the smell of burnt sugar from freshly made bibingka.

What made the trip even better was its simplicity. No complicated itinerary, no rush. Just good company, good food, and views that reminded us why local travel is worth it.

What made Vigan unforgettable wasn’t just its heritage—it was how alive it all felt. The past wasn’t frozen in time; it was living, breathing, cooked into every meal and paved into every street. We didn’t rush, we didn’t plan too much. We just wandered and listened.

Would I return? In a heartbeat. Because some places don’t just ask to be visited—they ask to be remembered.
