Where Time Slows Down: A Bohol Reverie


I found myself in Bohol, seeking something quieter, something softer than my usual pace. I wasn’t running away from the world, but maybe I was hoping to slow it down a bit. From the moment I crossed the waters to get there, Bohol welcomed me gently—with the golden glow of Panglao's shores, the hum of tricycles rolling down sleepy roads, and the distant laughter of children playing by the coast. I stood on the edge of Alona Beach at sunrise, coffee in hand, watching fishermen row out as the sky blushed pink and coral. Later that day, I took a cruise down the Loboc River, where the boat drifted slowly through lush green canopies and a folk band played songs I didn’t know but somehow understood.

But Bohol wasn’t just about peaceful moments—it had its own wild magic too. I gazed in awe at the surreal curves of the Chocolate Hills, felt like a child again on a bamboo hanging bridge, and met the wide-eyed tarsiers that stared back with curious calm. Each experience unraveled parts of me I hadn’t touched in a while—curiosity, lightness, stillness. Evenings were filled with grilled seafood, cold drinks, and conversations with strangers that felt like old friends. I remember lying on the sand one night, the stars in full bloom above, and thinking: maybe this is what healing looks like—quiet, warm, and completely unforced.

Before I left, I helped plant a mangrove seedling by the shore—a small gesture for a place that reminded me how to simply be. Bohol wasn’t loud about its beauty, but it whispered in ways that stayed with you long after you’re gone. I didn’t just see Bohol—I felt it.
